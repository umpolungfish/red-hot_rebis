## Summary

A short natural philosophical treatise from Bacon's unfinished *Instauratio Magna*, using the alchemical framework of Sulphur and Mercury as an organizing principle for classifying natural phenomena across four levels of reality (subterranean, vegetable/animal, pneumatic, celestial). Bacon reduces Paracelsus' tria prima to a duality (Sal is a compound of the other two) and identifies the "Version of Water into Oil" as the fundamental natural transformation — greater in nature than turning silver into gold.

**This is not an alchemical text in the operative sense.** It is a natural philosophy text that uses alchemical categories as its analytical framework.

### Tier: O₁

**Justification:** Bacon's text is structurally intermediate between an alchemical text and a natural philosophical treatise. It adopts the Sulphur/Mercury framework but uses it as a taxonomy, not an operative system. The text is O₁ because:
1. Systematic classification across four levels of reality
2. Explicit methodological framework (collecting instances → classifying → proposing mechanisms)
3. Universal scope (all bodies follow these laws)
4. Cites Paracelsus but moves beyond him — reducing tria prima to duality
---

## Key Structural Features

**1. Alchemical Categories as Taxonomy**
Bacon does not practice alchemy — he uses its categories as an analytical framework. This is a crucial structural shift. The Sulphur/Mercury duality becomes a lens for seeing nature, not a recipe for transforming it.

**2. Reduction from Triad to Dyad**
Paracelsus posited Tria Prima (Salt, Sulphur, Mercury). Bacon explicitly reduces this: "as for their Sal, which is their Third Principle, it is a Compound of the other two." This is a structural simplification — from three to two — with Sal as the composite.

**3. Version of Water into Oil as Fundamental**
The central thesis: "it is one of the greatest Magnalia Naturae, to turne Water or Watry Iuyce into Oile or Oily Juyce: Greater in Nature, than to turne Silver, or Quick-silver, into Gold." This reframes the alchemical project: the real philosopher's stone is not gold but the process by which nature turns crude into mature.

**4. Four Instances as Evidence Structure**
Bacon presents four kinds of evidence for the crude→oily transformation:
1. Mixture of Earth and Water → Nitrous Fatnesse (plants)
2. Assimilation of Nourishment (digestion in plants and animals)
3. Inception of Putrefaction (corrupted water, distilled waters)
4. Dulcoration of Metalls (Saccharum Saturni)

This is proto-scientific: collect instances, classify them, infer mechanism.

**5. Heat as Universal Operator**
"Oile is almost Nothing else but Water Disgested; And this Disgestion is principally by Heat." A single mechanism (heat-mediated digestion) explains all four kinds of transformation.

## Relation to the Corpus

Bacon's text is structurally closest to **Kircher's Table** (O₀) in being a meta-text about alchemy rather than a text of alchemy. But Bacon goes further: he uses alchemical categories as an analytical framework for natural philosophy, whereas Kircher merely inventories equipment.

Bacon's reduction of tria prima to a duality anticipates the modern chemical distinction between reducing and oxidizing agents, and his "Version of Water into Oil" is a proto-statement of the carbon cycle.
