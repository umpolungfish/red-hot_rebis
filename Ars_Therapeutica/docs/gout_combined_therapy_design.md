## 4. Mechanisms of Synergy

### 4.1 How Diet Amplifies FCAGT

The dietary pattern contributes to FCAGT efficacy through three independent mechanisms:

1. **Reduced substrate load.** Lower dietary purine intake and enhanced renal urate excretion mean there is less soluble urate competing for Domain 1 binding sites. FCAGT's Frobenius-closed domain (urate sensor, $\mu \circ \delta = \text{id}$) operates at lower occupancy, preserving sensitivity for the saturated micro-environment at crystal surfaces.

2. **Reduced NLRP3 priming.** Sleep, exercise, and anti-inflammatory dietary polyphenols (from cherries, berries, coffee) reduce baseline NLRP3 inflammasome priming. This means that even if crystal dissolution transiently releases MSU microcrystals, the inflammatory response is dampened. The IL-1$\beta$ trap payload has less work to do.

3. **Alkalinized urine.** High fruit/vegetable intake + high water intake raises urine pH and lowers urine specific gravity, reducing the risk of renal MSU nucleation during the period when serum urate is being driven down by FCAGT + diet together. This prevents the paradoxical "renal urate dumping" flare that can occur with urate-lowering monotherapy.

### 4.2 How FCAGT Enables Dietary Success

Conversely, FCAGT solves the two problems that make diet-alone approaches fail:

1. **The crystal burden problem.** A patient with years of accumulated MSU deposits cannot dissolve them through diet alone — urate is only sparingly soluble, and even perfect dietary adherence can only reduce serum urate by $\approx 2$–$3$ mg/dL. FCAGT directly dissolves existing deposits, clearing the "debt" so the dietary pattern only needs to maintain the "balance."

2. **The early-flare problem.** Urate-lowering therapy of any kind (including diet) can trigger acute flares in the first weeks as crystals begin to dissolve and shed micro-particles. The IL-1$\beta$ trap payload blocks this inflammatory response, making the dietary transition tolerable and improving adherence during the critical first 8 weeks.
