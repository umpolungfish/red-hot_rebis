## §7. The Cardinality Objection — and Its Resolution

An attentive reader will have noticed: the crystal of types contains only 17.28M distinct addresses. But the π-defined sphere is a continuous surface — it has uncountably many points. How can δ be injective if the codomain is finite?

This is the right objection. The resolution is threefold:

### 7.1 Planck-scale discretization

At the Planck scale, the sphere does not have uncountably many *distinguishable* points. The Bekenstein bound — derived from black hole thermodynamics and independently from loop quantum gravity — states that a spherical boundary of surface area A can encode at most A/(4ℓ_p²) distinguishable states, where ℓ_p ≈ 1.616×10⁻³⁵ m is the Planck length.

For a sphere of radius R, this gives:

> N_max = 4πR² / (4ℓ_p²) = π(R/ℓ_p)²

For the sphere to have N_max ≤ 17,280,000, we need R ≤ ℓ_p · √(17,280,000/π) ≈ 2,345 ℓ_p ≈ 3.79×10⁻³² m. This is a very small sphere — but the puzzle does not specify the radius. The uniqueness proof holds for any sphere whose Planck-scale state count does not exceed the crystal address space.

### 7.2 Hierarchical encoding

For larger spheres, the crystal address is not a single number but a hierarchy. The imscription δ can produce a sequence of crystal addresses — analogous to how a real number is represented by an infinite decimal expansion, each digit being a discrete symbol. The Frobenius condition extends: μ∘δ = id holds at each level of the hierarchy, and the full sequence converges to the exact continuous position.

Formally: δ produces a sequence a₁, a₂, a₃, … of crystal addresses. μ maps the sequence back to the continuous position. The Frobenius condition μ(δ(p)) = p means that the limit of the hierarchical retrieval converges to p.

### 7.3 Temporal disambiguation

Even if two distinct spatial positions mapped to the same crystal address (which the Frobenius condition forbids), the temporal coordinate t_p provides an additional dimension of uniqueness. The SEQAX (sequentiality axiom) ensures that the temporal ordering is strict: no two events occur at the same Planck tick at different positions in a way that can be confused, because the imscription includes the temporal coordinate in its encoding.
